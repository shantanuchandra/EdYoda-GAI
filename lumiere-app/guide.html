A Field Guide to AI Agents · No. 07 One brain, many channels
The build guide · ~6 min read

How this bakery bot was built — with no code.

You just watched it take a cake order, price it, and reply. Here's exactly how it works under the hood — in plain English, no jargon you need to look up.

Thanks for trying the demo. This is the short version of how it's wired — and how you could build one yourself.

The one idea that makes it work

Most people, when they want a chatbot on three places — a website, email, WhatsApp — build three chatbots. That's three things to update every time the menu changes.

This bot does the opposite. There's one brain that thinks, and the channels are just doors into it. A message comes in from any door, the same brain handles it, and the answer goes back out the door it came in on.

EmailTelegramWebsite →one brain→ reply on the same door

The brain never learns what a "channel" is. A tiny tag — channel: "web" — rides along with each message and decides which door to answer through. That's the whole trick.

The four pieces

Nothing here is a server you have to run or babysit. Four moving parts, three of them free to start:

  • A static website — just a file, hosted free (this one's on Vercel). The chat box lives here.
  • n8n — a no-code automation tool. You drag boxes onto a canvas and connect them. This is where the "brain" lives.
  • A large language model — Google's Gemini, on its free tier. It reads the order and writes the reply.
  • A tiny pricing function — the one bit of actual code (~100 lines), and you could skip even that by letting the model quote from a price list.

How a single order flows

When you typed "2kg eggless red velvet for Saturday," here's what happened in the ~4 seconds before it replied:

1

The website sends your message to n8n

The chat box does one thing: it bundles up your message plus the conversation so far and posts it to a webhook — basically a private URL that wakes up the n8n workflow. Sending the history each time is the secret to memory: that's why "2kg of that" still knew you meant red velvet.

2

Gemini reads the order

Inside n8n, the message goes to Gemini with a set of instructions: you're a bakery assistant, here's the menu and the rules, figure out what they want. Gemini pulls out the flavour, size, date, and address — and notices if something's missing, so it can ask.

3

A small function prices it

Once the order's complete, n8n calls the little pricing function — flavour rate × size, plus the eggless surcharge. That's where the exact ₹2,180 came from. Keeping the maths in code (not the model) means the price is always right, never hallucinated.

4

The reply goes back out the right door

n8n checks the channel tag and sends the answer back the way it came — into this chat box for you, or as an email / Telegram message for someone else. Same brain, different door. Adding a fourth channel later is just adding a fourth door — the brain doesn't change.

The honest part

This is a teaching demo — a real AI on a made-up bakery. The pricing, the rules, the lead capture are all real working pieces; "Lumière" isn't a real shop. The point isn't the cake. It's that this shape — one trigger, a model, an action — is the backbone of most useful AI automations.

If you wanted to build your own

The honest truth: the tools have gotten easy enough that the tech isn't the hard part anymore. The hard part is just starting. A rough first version of something like this is a weekend, not a degree:

  • Make a free n8n account and a free Gemini API key.
  • Drag a Webhook node, a Gemini node, and a "respond" node onto the canvas.
  • Write the model's instructions in plain English — what it is, what it should do, what it must refuse.
  • Point a chat box (or your email, or a Telegram bot) at the webhook. Done.
